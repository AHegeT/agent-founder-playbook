Playbook Writing Guide: Agents & Founders

This guide defines the standard for creating high-value playbooks for the Agents & Founders community. These documents are not blog posts; they are operational manuals for building systems.

1. Core Identity

The Audience

Who they are: SMB Founders, Builders, and Operators.

Their Mindset: They are tech-savvy enough to understand "agents" and "API," but they are business-focused. They don't want code snippets; they want business outcomes (saved time, higher margin, better hires).

Their Constraint: They are extremely busy. They will skim. If they don't find value in 30 seconds, they close the tab.

The Tone

Style: "Engineer-to-Engineer" but pragmatic.

Vibe: Direct, systematic, and low-fluff. It should feel like a leaked internal document from a smart company, not a generic marketing article.

Voice: Authoritative but peer-driven. We use "we" to refer to the community insights.

Forbidden: Fluff, corporate jargon, overly salesy language, and generic advice (e.g., "Communication is key").

The Format

Length: 800 – 1,000 words maximum.

Structure: Markdown (.md).

Visuals: Heavy use of bullet points, bold text for emphasis, and tables for comparison. No long paragraphs (keep them under 3-4 lines).

2. The Strategic Framework

Every playbook must follow the "Profile Maturity Model". We do not give one-size-fits-all advice. We segment solutions by the user's stage.

The 3 Profiles

Always define these three specific avatars for the topic at hand:

The Builder (Stage 1): The person starting from zero or cleaning up a mess. Needs foundational tools and hygiene.

The Optimizer (Stage 2): The person drowning in manual work. Needs automation, agents, and friction reduction.

The Strategist (Stage 3): The person scaling. Needs forecasting, insights, and decision intelligence.

3. Structural Requirements

Every playbook must contain these five specific sections in this order:

I. The Hook (Introduction)

The "Old Way": Call out the painful status quo (e.g., "Bank Balance Accounting").

The "New Way": Introduce the system metaphor (e.g., "The Automated CFO").

The Philosophy: One bold, contrarian insight that anchors the piece (e.g., "Start by the End").

II. The Diagnostic (Table)

A clear | Profile | Pain | Goal | table that lets the reader self-select their section immediately.

III. The Execution (The "How-To" Profiles)

Break down the solution for each profile.

The Rule: Use the "Workflow Syntax" for instructions:

Standard: "Connect X to Y."

Playbook Style: Trigger $\rightarrow$ Action $\rightarrow$ Result.

Specific Tools: Always name specific tools (e.g., "Dext," "n8n"), not just categories ("OCR tools").

IV. The Anti-Patterns (Common Mistakes)

List 3 specific traps people fall into.

Format as Mistake vs. Fix.

Example: Mistake: "Building a dashboard." Fix: "Building an alert."

V. The Toolkit (Curated Stack)

A list of the specific software/resources discussed.

Group by user type (e.g., "For the Architect," "For the Operator").

4. Writing Checklist

Before publishing, verify:

[ ] Scannability: Can I understand the main point just by reading the headers and bold text?

[ ] Actionability: Is there a specific tool or workflow mentioned?

[ ] Profile Check: Did I give advice for the beginner and the expert?

[ ] Metric: Does the header promise a specific result (e.g., "Save 10 hours")?
